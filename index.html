Featured Project

SEAL Team Project: Adaptive Management Plan for Seals near Churchill, Manitoba

Project Overview

This project was completed as part of the Students Engaged in Applied Learning (SEAL) Team at the University of Manitoba. The team included Paige A. Newcomb, Emma R. Burton, Conner R. O’Dowda, and Dev K. Dave. The final project was built through shared research, planning, discussion, writing, mapping, editing, and presentation work, and I want to give equal credit to the full team.

Our project focused on bearded seals, ringed seals, and harbour seals near Churchill, Manitoba. We created an adaptive management plan designed to respond to climate change, shipping pressure, and tourism-related disturbance while also improving long-term population monitoring and management planning.

The project aimed to move beyond observation alone. It proposed a practical framework for monitoring seal populations, reducing anthropogenic harm, involving communities, and supporting better conservation decisions over time.

Why This Work Matters

The report recognized that seals are essential components of the western Hudson Bay ecosystem but often receive less attention than larger Arctic species. We highlighted how sea ice loss, disturbance from tourism, and vessel traffic can affect feeding, moulting, pupping, behaviour, and overall resilience.

Main Objectives

Our plan had two major goals. First, it aimed to support long-term population stability through baseline abundance research and continued monitoring. Second, it aimed to reduce human-caused disturbance by recommending improvements to shipping practices and ecotourism behaviour in the Churchill region.

Research and Monitoring Design

The project proposed initial population research followed by a repeating five-year monitoring cycle. For bearded and ringed seals, the plan recommended aerial visual direct count surveys with photographic support. For harbour seals, it recommended land-based visual counts supported by telemetry and local knowledge.

We also emphasized adaptive management, meaning the methods should remain scientifically consistent but still allow improvement if better approaches, such as drones or other efficient techniques, become feasible.

Study Area and Indigenous Involvement

The selected observational area extended from the Seal River in the north to Fort Churchill in the south and included both marine and freshwater environments relevant to all three seal species. This area was chosen because it also overlaps with tourism and shipping pressures.

Indigenous involvement was a central part of the plan. The report emphasized collaboration that respects traditional ecological knowledge, local priorities, and equitable participation in planning and monitoring.

Shipping Recommendations

One of the strongest parts of the plan focused on shipping impacts. We proposed buffer zones, ship speed reduction near seal-sensitive areas and ports, and the use of AIS-based shipping data to monitor vessel activity. These actions were intended to reduce underwater noise, disturbance, and ice-related disruption.

Tourism Recommendations

The project also recommended more conservation-focused tourism practices. These included greater viewing distance, more careful use of vehicles and boats, and stronger protections during vulnerable periods such as pupping and haul-out times. The goal was to reduce stress and behavioural disruption while still allowing responsible wildlife viewing.

Budget and Phased Plan

The report proposed an estimated budget of about $2 million CAD to support surveys, data analysis, community-based monitoring, and technology related to shipping management. It recommended a two-phase rollout, beginning with data collection and early regulations and then moving toward stronger long-term management actions based on results.

What I Took From It

This project strengthened my interest in wildlife conservation, environmental stewardship, and practical management planning. It showed me how field ecology, stakeholder thinking, research, mapping, and policy awareness can come together in one collaborative conservation project.

The Wildlife Society

TWS Annual General Meeting and Joint Conference

This spring, I attended The Wildlife Society Annual General Meeting and Joint Conference as part of my involvement with the Manitoba Chapter. As Secretary, I had important responsibilities before and during the event, especially around organization, record keeping, and helping the event run smoothly.

This experience gave me a stronger appreciation for how conferences create space for professionals, students, and conservation-minded people to exchange knowledge and build community.

UM Wetlanders

Duck Box Maintenance Along the University River Bank

I also took part in an activity with the UM Wetlanders group where we worked along the university’s river bank to help maintain duck boxes that had been installed a few years ago. This was a hands-on habitat stewardship activity focused on preparing the nesting boxes for the next breeding season.

Our work involved opening the boxes, cleaning out old hay and straw, removing unhatched eggs, and replenishing the boxes with fresh straw, hay, and woodchips so they would be ready for this year’s ducks. It was a simple but meaningful conservation task that showed how consistent maintenance supports wildlife habitat over time.

I enjoyed being part of work that directly supports local bird populations in a practical and visible way. Experiences like this remind me that conservation is not only about large-scale policy or research, but also about regular care, attention, and follow-through in the field.

Delta Waterfowl

Mentored Waterfowl Hunting Experience

Last fall, I participated in a Delta Waterfowl mentored hunting experience, where I learned about the process of waterfowl hunting with shotguns in a structured and educational setting. The trip was an overnight experience and introduced me to the practical, ethical, and historical dimensions of this tradition.

A major part of the experience was learning about ethical hunting practices, the history behind waterfowl hunting, and the role that hunters have played in the North American model of conservation. I gained a deeper understanding of how conservation, regulation, and responsible harvest have developed together over time.

After the hunt, we also learned how to clean the birds respectfully and use them without waste. That part of the experience stayed with me because it emphasized responsibility, respect for wildlife, and the importance of understanding the full process rather than treating it casually.
